What is it?

TNUoS — Transmission Network Use of System — is what everyone pays for the high-voltage grid itself: the pylons, cables and substations that move power across the country. The network is being expanded at historic pace, substantially to connect wind farms far from where the power is used.

Why does it exist?

The transmission network has always been paid for through bills. What has changed is the size of the build-out: connecting remote renewable generation requires new lines that a fossil-fuel system concentrated near cities did not.

What has it cost?

since 2002, currently growing at about a year — every second. Spread across Britain's households, that is about per household per year, paid through electricity bills.

How sure are we?

The total amount of transmission revenue is measured directly from NESO's official charging statements — that figure is solid. What is estimated is the share attributable to renewables. The rule is conservative: only the growth in transmission revenue above a CPIH-indexed pre-renewables baseline (the 2002–05 average, roughly £0.9bn a year) is counted, and the uplift is floored at zero in any year where revenue falls below that baseline. This means the figures here are the attributed uplift, not the raw TNUoS total — most of the network charge on your bill is not counted. Attribution confidence for this component is low: the transmission build-out has many drivers, and the baseline is a judgement call.
