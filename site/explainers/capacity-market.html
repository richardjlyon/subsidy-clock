What is it?

The Capacity Market pays power stations — mostly gas — simply to exist and be ready to generate when called upon. It is insurance against the lights going out on still, dark evenings when wind and solar produce little.

Why does it exist?

A grid with large amounts of weather-dependent generation needs guaranteed backup for the hours when that generation disappears. The Capacity Market buys that guarantee years in advance through auctions, and the cost is levied on suppliers.

What has it cost?

since 2016, currently growing at about a year — every second. Spread across Britain's households, that is about per household per year, paid through electricity bills.

Illustrative bill, not your tariff. Suppliers pay in proportion to their customers' demand on winter evenings and pass it into bills.

How sure are we?

Capacity Market settlement payments are measured from LCCC's published data — the total is solid. What is estimated is the attribution to renewables. The rule counts the full levy: availability payments exist to procure firm backup capacity for a system with a growing share of intermittent generation, so the whole cost is attributed. The caveat is that some backup capacity would be procured in any system — the full-attribution approach therefore errs high rather than low. Attribution confidence is medium.
