What is it?

Under the UK Emissions Trading Scheme, electricity generators must buy an allowance for every tonne of CO₂ they emit. Generators price those allowances into the electricity they sell, so the cost flows into the wholesale price of power — and into your bill.

Why does it exist?

Carbon pricing is the textbook tool for cutting emissions: make emitting expensive and cleaner options win on price. The revenue goes to the Treasury.

What has it cost?

since 2009, currently growing at about a year — every second. Spread across Britain's households, that is about per household per year, reaching you through the wholesale price of power. Only the power-sector share of auction revenue is counted here.

How sure are we?

Auction revenue is measured from published UK ETS auction results — the total is solid. What is estimated is the power sector's share. The rule scales auction revenue by the power sector's share of verified covered emissions. Power generation receives no free allocation, so it buys a larger share of auctioned allowances than its share of total covered emissions — the figures here are therefore a lower bound on what the power sector actually pays. This is also where the Clock is stricter than REF's reconstruction, which counts ETS receipts across all sectors: only the power sector's share of auctioned allowances is counted here. Attribution confidence is medium.
