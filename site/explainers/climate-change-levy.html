What is it?

The Climate Change Levy is a tax on the energy used by businesses and the public sector, paired with Carbon Price Support, a tax on the fuels used to generate electricity. Companies pay it on their bills; generators pay it on their gas and coal. Both costs end up in the prices of electricity, goods and services.

Why does it exist?

Both taxes exist to make carbon-emitting energy more expensive than low-carbon energy — a deliberate price signal to push businesses and generators away from fossil fuels.

What has it cost?

since 2002, currently growing at about a year — every second. Spread across Britain's households, that is about per household per year. Only the electricity share of the levy is counted here.

Illustrative bill, not your tariff. Households do not pay CCL directly; it reaches you through the price of everything made and sold using taxed energy.

How sure are we?

Total CCL receipts are measured from HMRC's published levy declarations — the amount is solid. What is estimated is the share attributable to making the switch to low-carbon energy worthwhile. The rule is deliberately narrow: only electricity's share of main-rate CCL declarations is counted — gas and solid-fuel CCL is excluded — plus Carbon Price Support, which falls wholly on electricity generation. The figure errs low: gas and other fuel CCL that also incentivises switching away from fossil fuels is left out entirely, and because HMRC's calendar-year CCL/CPF split begins in 2014, the CPF component is omitted entirely for 2013 — its first year — understating that year too. Attribution confidence is medium.
