What is it?

Constraint payments are money paid to wind farms to switch off. When the wires cannot carry the power — most often from Scottish wind farms to English demand — the grid operator pays the wind farm to stop generating, and usually pays a gas station elsewhere to start. The wind farm is compensated for the subsidy it would have earned; you pay for electricity that was never made.

Why does it exist?

Wind farms were built faster than the transmission lines to carry their output. Until the network catches up, paying generators to stop is the grid operator's only way to keep the system in balance — and the cost lands on bills.
