What is it?

Balancing Services Use of System charges pay the grid operator's minute-to-minute costs of keeping supply and demand exactly equal: reserve power, frequency response, and paying stations to turn up or down. A system with more variable wind and solar needs more of this.

Why does it exist?

Every grid needs balancing. What this page counts is the increase above the cost of balancing the old, fossil-dominated system — the uplift attributable to running a renewables-heavy grid.

What has it cost?

since 2002, currently growing at about a year — every second. Spread across Britain's households, that is about per household per year, paid through electricity bills.

How sure are we?

Total BSUoS costs are measured from NESO's published daily balancing data — the amount is solid. What is estimated is the share attributable to renewables. The rule mirrors TNUoS: only the uplift above a CPIH-indexed pre-renewables baseline (the 2002–05 average, roughly £0.4bn a year) is counted, floored at zero in any year where costs fall below that baseline. There is also a double-counting guard: constraint payments to wind farms are counted in the direct layer ("paid to switch off"), and those same payments are recovered from bill-payers through BSUoS, so the BSUoS series is netted of direct constraint payments before attribution — the same pound is never counted twice. Attribution confidence is low: balancing costs depend on many factors beyond the renewables share, and the baseline is a judgement call.
