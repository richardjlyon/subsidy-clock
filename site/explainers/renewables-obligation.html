What is it?

The Renewables Obligation is the single largest renewable-energy subsidy in Britain. From 2002 to 2017, wind farms, solar farms and other renewable generators were awarded certificates (ROCs) for every unit of electricity they produced. Energy suppliers are legally required to buy these certificates, and they pass that cost on to you. The scheme closed to new generators in 2017, but certificates keep being issued to existing ones — payments continue until 2037.

Why does it exist?

In 2002, renewable electricity cost far more to generate than electricity from gas or coal. The government chose not to pay the difference from taxes, but to oblige suppliers — and therefore billpayers — to fund it. The aim was to get wind and solar built at scale by guaranteeing their owners an income above the market price of power.

What has it cost?

since 2002, currently growing at about a year — every second. Spread across Britain's households, that is about per household per year, paid through electricity bills.

Illustrative bill, not your tariff. From April 2026 the government is funding 75% of Renewables Obligation costs from general taxation rather than bills — a temporary measure running until March 2029 — so the RO levy remaining on bills is under 1p/kWh, down from roughly 3p before. The Renewables Obligation never appears as a named line: it is folded into the unit price of every kWh you buy. You cannot see it, and you cannot opt out of it.
